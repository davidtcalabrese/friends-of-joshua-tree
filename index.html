Joshua Tree

The Park is named for the Joshua Tree, Yucca Brevifolia which is native to the Mojave Desert. It was named Mormon settlers who encountered it while traversing the country in the mid nineteenth century. The tree's shape reminded them of the biblical story of Joshua in which he reaches his hands to the heavens in prayer.

Conservation Status

Due to climate change as well as other factors, the Joshua Trees, which have grown in the region for two and a half million years, are at risk of disappearing.

Studies have suggested that Joshua Tree National Park may soon have no Joshua Trees, with their population likely being reduced by 90% by the end of the current century.

In 2020, environmentalists filed a petition to the state government to grant the tree protection under the California Endangered Species Act. In September, 2020, the state granted a temporary protection of the Joshua Trees while the case is being reviewed. This means it is illegal to damage or remove a Joshua Tree without special permission.

COVID Travel Suggestions

We're open during the COVID pandemic. We follow the CDC guidelines which recommends people maintain six feet of distance at all times. Wear a mask when this is not possible.

Staying Safe at Joshua Tree Park

Extreme heat and aggressive animals pose a potential threat, but you'll be safe if you follow the park's protocol. Pack plenty of water stay on the trails and bring a map.

Activities for Kids and Families

Joshua Tree Park includes tons of activities for people of all ages. Encourage your children to keep an eye out for wildlife or organize a scavenger hunt.
